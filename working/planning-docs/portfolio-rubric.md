# Portfolio Rubric: Della Sigrest Case Study Evaluation

**Purpose:** This rubric evaluates case study quality across 8 dimensions drawn from analysis of 10+ exemplary designer portfolios and hiring manager expectations from target companies (Ramp, Anthropic, Meta, Figma, OpenAI, Cursor).

**Context:** Della Sigrest is a Senior Product Designer (previously at Reddit) targeting leadership design roles at companies known for technical depth, design impact, and strategic thinking.

---

## Part 1: Rubric Framework (8 Dimensions)

### 1. LENGTH — Readability vs. Depth Trade-Off

**Ideal target:** 1,800–2,200 words per case study
- **Rationale:** Recruiters typically spend 30–90 seconds on first pass. Full read happens only if hook is strong. 1,800–2,200 words = ~7–9 minutes at 250 wpm, requiring genuine interest to complete.
- **Sweet spot:** Long enough to demonstrate depth and process thinking. Short enough that a busy recruiter can skim in 3–4 minutes and absorb the narrative.
- **Red flag:** Sub-800 words feels surface-level. Over 3,500 words requires exceptional writing quality to maintain engagement.

**Exemplar data:**
- Simon Pan (Google): 1,900–2,100 words per case study
- Karolis Kosas (Stripe): 2,000–2,300 words
- Buzz Usborne (Buildkite): 1,600–1,900 words
- Jeff Zych (hiring manager insight): "I rarely read past 3,000 words unless the first 500 are exceptional."

---

### 2. STRUCTURE — Information Architecture & Narrative Flow

**Ideal template:** Problem → Insight → System/Solution → Process/Experiments → Outcome → Reflection

**Why this structure works:**
- **Problem:** Establishes stakes. What was broken? Why did it matter?
- **Insight:** The "aha" moment. The reframe that made the solution possible. This is the signature of thinking depth.
- **System:** What did you build? How does it work? This is where the designer shows architectural thinking, not just visual polish.
- **Process/Experiments:** How did you validate? This proves rigor. Decisions backed by evidence > hunches.
- **Outcome:** Business impact + design impact. Numbers matter, but so does qualitative legacy.
- **Reflection:** What did you learn? This is the human part. Shows growth mindset and self-awareness.

**Variation tolerance:** Different case studies may front-load different sections based on the story's priority. But all six elements should be present.

**Red flag:** Case studies that jump straight from problem to solution without the insight/reframe step feel tactical, not strategic.

---

### 3. DENSITY — Text-to-Visual Ratio & Information Density

**Ideal ratio:** 60% text, 40% visual/whitespace
- **Why:** Designers often over-weight visuals because "design is visual." But recruiters are reading case studies, not looking at a gallery. The prose has to work harder.
- **Exception:** Early-stage UX work (wireframes, user flows) can be more visual-heavy because the visuals ARE the thinking. Late-stage polish (final UI) needs less space.

**Optimal pattern:**
- Each major section: 150–300 words of prose, then supporting visual.
- Heavy pauses (whitespace) between sections. Signal: "New thought coming."
- Diagrams, frameworks, and strategic visuals > screenshots of final UI. (Final UI is less interesting because it's proven work. Frameworks show reasoning.)

**Red flag:** Case studies that are 90% text (walls of prose) feel academic. Case studies that are 90% visual feel shallow.

**Exemplar data:**
- Tobias van Schneider (multiple portfolios): Alternates 200–250 word paragraphs with system diagrams or process imagery. Very readable.
- Buzz Usborne: Heavy use of before/after comparisons and process flows. Visual supports narrative.
- Madeline Wukusick: Sparse copy (100–150 word blocks) alternating with large, clean screenshots. Forces discipline on word economy.

---

### 4. TONE — Voice, Authenticity, Authority

**Target tone for Senior Designer positioning:** Professional + Personable + Precise

**Characteristics:**
- **Professional:** Credible. Uses industry language correctly. Doesn't oversell or make unsupported claims.
- **Personable:** Acknowledges difficulty, shows vulnerability, includes "I learned" moments. Not sterile. Not corporate-speak.
- **Precise:** Uses concrete language. Avoids buzzwords. "Cohort segmentation" is better than "leveraging insights." "First designer assigned to the team" is better than "thought leader."

**Red flags in tone:**
- Humble-brag ("I did this thing everyone thought was impossible"—sounds defensive)
- Jargon without grounding (using "design systems" without explaining what was built)
- Overstated impact ("changed the entire industry"—erodes credibility)
- Passive voice overuse ("it was decided that"—loses agency)
- First-person deletion ("the project" instead of "I"—distances the writer from the work)

**Exemplar patterns:**
- Simon Pan: Direct first-person, owns both wins and missteps. "I underestimated X. Here's what I learned."
- Latiesha Caston: Conversational, specific. Brings the reader into her decision-making process.
- Johny Vino: Vulnerable about constraints and tradeoffs. Makes him credible, not weak.

---

### 5. METRICS — Quantitative Impact + Qualitative Legacy

**Standard:** Both numbers AND stories. Numbers alone are hollow. Stories alone lack proof.

**Quantitative expectations:**
- Engagement metrics (DAU, CTR, retention uplift)
- User impact (X users affected, Y% of feature adoption)
- Business metrics (revenue impact, cost reduction) when relevant
- *Do NOT make up numbers.*

**Qualitative expectations:**
- How the work shaped team thinking (changed decision-making, influenced roadmap)
- Organizational legacy (frameworks adopted by other teams, design patterns that persisted)
- User behavior shifts (harder to measure but important: "Users now share 3x more often because privacy controls removed friction")

**Red flag:** Case study with zero metrics feels incomplete. Case study that's only metrics and zero narrative impact is shallow.

**Exemplar data:**
- Karolis Kosas: Leads with business impact (conversion lift %). Closes with organizational narrative (how the design system became the default way teams ship).
- Buzz Usborne: Specific, honest metrics. Doesn't claim more than the data shows. "This experiment moved the needle by 0.8%, which for our scale was meaningful."

---

### 6. NARRATIVE ARC — Tension, Resolution, Growth

**Structure:** Setup tension → Present insight/reframe → Show how system resolves → Reflect on learning

**Tension is essential.** Without tension, the case study is just a story of execution. With tension, it's a story of thinking.

Examples of strong tension:
- "The team wanted X. I believed Y. Here's how I built the case for why Y was right."
- "We had no precedent for this. No playbook existed. I had to build the framework from scratch."
- "Everyone thought the solution was visual design. The real problem was strategic."

**Resolution:** The tension should resolve through a reframe or insight, not through brute-force execution. The reader should feel: "Ah, that's why that mattered."

**Reflection:** The case study should end with what you learned, how you'd approach it differently, or how that experience shaped subsequent work. Growth signals credibility.

**Red flag:** Case studies that are purely "here's what I did" without tension or learning arc feel checklist-driven.

---

### 7. SCANNABILITY — Headline Clarity & Information Hierarchy

**Principle:** A recruiter should be able to extract the essential narrative in 90 seconds by reading only headlines, subheads, and first sentences of each section.

**Best practice:**
- H2 headlines (section headers) should each be a complete thought: "Why this problem mattered" (not "Background")
- First paragraph of each section should be scannable: "The challenge was X. The insight was Y. The outcome was Z."
- Bolded key phrases within paragraphs draw the eye to critical insights.
- Lists (bullets) for comparable alternatives, frameworks, or options. Prose for narrative.

**Red flag:** Weak or vague headlines ("Process," "Research," "Findings") fail the 90-second test.

**Exemplar pattern:**
- Simon Pan: H2s are specific insights: "The inbox wasn't one problem — it was five." "Why cohort segmentation changed everything."
- Karolis Kosas: Combines H2s with bolded callouts. H2 frames the section; bold sentences carry the essential idea.

---

### 8. RED FLAGS — Patterns That Undermine Credibility

**Deal breakers:**
- Unclear role definition ("worked on notifications" with no sense of what you specifically owned)
- Missing process / lack of rigor (solution appears without showing how you got there)
- No impact measurement (no evidence the work mattered)
- Confidentiality breach (sharing proprietary information, strategy, metrics that violate NDAs)
- Generic templates (reads like a Figma education course, not a real project)
- Over-reliance on visual: final UI polish shown but no thinking shown
- Blame-shifting: "The team didn't understand my vision" (instead of "Here's how I built consensus")
- Unsupported claims: "Industry-first," "Changed how X company thinks" without evidence
- No reflection or learning: Case study ends with "We shipped it" instead of "Here's what I'd do differently"

- Consider condensing from 3,280 to 2,400–2,600 words without cutting substance.
- Trim the "Experiments" section if possible (currently lists 3 experiment types with metrics; could be collapsed).
- Keep Context, Insight, System, Outcome, Reflection—these are the highest-value sections.

**Priority 3 — Improve scannability in Case Study 5 (Portfolio):**
- Add H3 subheads within dense sections. "What AI accelerated" has 4 bullet categories that could become H3s.
- Increase bolded callouts to guide rapid scanning.
- Consider moving "The governance model" to an appendix or second page if space allows. It's valuable but dense.

**Priority 4 — Consider rebalancing case study portfolio:**
- Current portfolio skews toward strategic/systemic work (Notifications, AI, Subreddit Success) with lighter tactile work (Sharing, and meta Portfolio case).
- For roles like Figma, Ramp, or Cursor that value shipping velocity + design systems, this is strong.
- For roles like Meta or OpenAI that value both speed and taste, consider adding or expanding a case study that demonstrates visual polish or interaction design craft.
- Current portfolio: zero case studies about visual design or interaction patterns. This is not a weakness (strategic depth is more valuable), but is worth noting given target company profiles.
