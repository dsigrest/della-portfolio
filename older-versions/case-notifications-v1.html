Unified Inbox

Shipped unified notifications architecture. +1.4% DAU, +6.4% CTR on suggested actions.

[Architecture diagram: how unified inbox integrates across Reddit surfaces]

Context

Users received notifications across 5+ surfaces: inbox, modqueue, mod alerts, home timeline, and on-page badges. Each surface owned its notification format and state machine. Notifications were inconsistent in tone, latency, action surface, and delivery rules.

The team hypothesis: surface consistency and a unified action layer would increase action rate and DAU.

[Before: fragmented notification surfaces across Reddit]

The Design

I architected a unified inbox system that separated concerns across three layers:

  • Data layer: Unified event schema and notification generation pipeline (owned by backend)
  • Storage & state: Single source of truth for notification read/dismiss state, with optimistic updates
  • UI layer: Reusable notification card component with flexible metadata slots

This allowed us to ship consistent UX while teams moved at their own pace to adopt the new feed.

[Notification card component variants and states]

What Worked

The architecture scaled. Over 18 months, 8 surfaces migrated to unified inbox without backend redesign. Adoption was gradual and low-risk—teams used the new component but kept their existing data sources at first.

Metrics: +1.4% DAU, +6.4% CTR on suggested actions. Action surface unification was the main driver; users were more likely to act when suggestions appeared in a consistent, familiar format.

The Failure

Launch required an extra click to see action buttons (icon → expand → button). Early adoption was low. Telemetry showed users never expanded. I reframed: instead of hiding buttons, I made action buttons visible by default and used copy to drive the behavior change. CTR on actions doubled. The constraint wasn't the feature—it was the action surface.

What I Learned

Architecture decisions compound over time. Early investment in separating concerns—schema, state, UI—meant downstream teams could move fast. Constraints often point to real user friction, not design polish.
