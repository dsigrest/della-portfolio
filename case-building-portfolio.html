Building This Portfolio

Designing with AI end-to-end — using Claude for research synthesis, content strategy, code generation, and iterative design review. A meta case study proving the practice.

Senior Product Designer
Personal Practice
2026
Web (Static HTML/CSS/JS)

The brief

My resume and LinkedIn had been updated multiple times — v4 and v14, respectively — but the portfolio was missing. The hiring narrative claimed AI-forward design practice, yet had no artifact-backed proof. Recruiters wanted evidence, not assertions.

The challenge was dual: build a portfolio that demonstrates the skills and judgment senior design roles require, while using that project itself as the proof point for AI-integrated workflow. The portfolio needed to close specific hiring gaps. Ramp required evidence of AI tools in design workflow. Meta wanted documented AI tool integration. Anthropic wanted code prototyping evidence. The artifact had to do the work the resume couldn't.

Goal: Create a portfolio that IS the evidence. Not a portfolio about AI-forward design. A portfolio designed with AI, end-to-end, with transparent governance and human review gates at every decision.

The approach

This wasn't a black-box autopilot project. The workflow was deliberate collaboration:

  • Research synthesis: Claude evaluated job descriptions from 6 target companies (Ramp, Anthropic, Meta, Figma, OpenAI, Cursor) and extracted the specific capabilities and signals each role required. That framework shaped what stories to tell and how to position them.
  • Content architecture: Working through narrative structure, information hierarchy, and case study arc. The portfolio tells a story about how I think — not just what I've built.
  • Copywriting and iteration: Rapid generation of tone variants, headline options, and messaging approaches. I kept what resonated. Discarded what didn't.
  • Code generation: HTML, CSS, and JavaScript built from scratch. No frameworks. No templates. Every line was reviewed before deploy.
  • Human review gates: At every decision point — narrative direction, tone, visual design choices, what to include and exclude — I approved or redirected. The human decided. The AI proposed.

This is not a case study about using ChatGPT to write copy faster. It's about integrating AI into the craft of design decision-making itself.

What AI accelerated

Specific, honest breakdown of where AI added value:

  • Research synthesis: Extracting 50+ job requirements across 6 companies would have taken days. Claude synthesized the themes in hours. The recruiter panel evaluation framework was rigorous because there was time to be rigorous.
  • Content architecture: Exploring 4–5 different case study structures, information hierarchies, and narrative arcs. Rapid iteration on "what's the core insight worth telling?" without writing fatigue setting in.
  • Code generation: The HTML/CSS/JS backbone came from prompts. I specified structure, reviewed output, caught bugs, refined. The site went from concept to deployed in weeks instead of months.
  • Iteration velocity: Copy variants, layout options, design token exploration. High volume of options meant higher confidence in final choices because I'd seen the alternatives.
  • Continuity across sessions: Build logs, prompt documentation, and version tracking meant I could pause and resume without losing context or repeating work.

Where human judgment was essential

Equally important: where AI's output became input to distinctly human decision-making.

  • Strategic direction: Which stories to tell. How to position Reddit's work given confidentiality constraints. What omit entirely because it didn't serve the narrative. This was never a question AI could answer alone.
  • Tone and voice: Authenticity matters. Recruiting isn't marketing. The portfolio needed to sound like me — direct, precise, reflective — not corporate or over-polished. I rewrote large passages to recover voice that got lost in iteration.
  • Visual design: Color palette, typography, layout decisions. AI proposed. I decided. The design is intentionally restrained because restraint signals confidence. That was a judgment call.
  • Confidentiality: What could be shown about past work. What needed redaction or generalization. This is a business and ethical judgment, not a design one.
  • Quality judgment: Knowing when "good enough" is actually good enough. Knowing what needs another pass. AI doesn't have taste — I do.

The governance model

Here's how the collaboration actually worked, session to session:

Prompt → Generate → Review → Iterate. I'd define a task (e.g., "Write the case study structure for the Notifications redesign"). Claude generated options. I reviewed for accuracy, tone, and strategic fit. If it missed the mark, I redirected with more specificity. If it was close, I iterated on tone or emphasis. If it was strong, it went into the build log and moved to the next task.

Human approval was required before any content went live. The AI proposed. The human disposed. That was especially true for strategic and taste decisions — things that require editorial judgment or could expose confidential information.

A build log tracked each session: what was generated, what was kept, what was reworked, and why. This served two purposes: accountability (I could explain every choice) and continuity (I could resume weeks later without losing context).

Code as proof

This portfolio is built in static HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No React. No Next.js. No templates. No frameworks.

Why? Because I prototype in both Figma and code depending on fidelity needs. High-level explorations happen in Figma. When I need to test interaction, responsive behavior, or performance, I move to code. This site required both: the visual system came from Figma; the implementation came from HTML/CSS/JS because the experience depends on the code.

Stats: ~560 lines of CSS, 6 HTML pages, fully responsive, accessible (WCAG AA), deployed on Vercel. No build step required. This is not a portfolio designed by a designer using a website builder. This is a portfolio designed by someone who codes.

For senior product roles that value prototyping velocity and technical literacy, this matters. Code-based prototyping is a natural extension of design practice, not a separate skill that "someone else" owns.

Outcome

What this process produced:

  • A portfolio site designed and deployed in weeks, not months — velocity that matters when you're job searching.
  • A recruiter panel evaluation framework calibrated against real job postings from Ramp, Anthropic, Meta, Figma, OpenAI, and Cursor.
  • A 5-phase development plan (research, strategy, content, code, deploy) reviewed for senior engineering quality and rigor.
  • A documented AI collaboration workflow that can be referenced in interviews — proof that I can use AI strategically in product work.
  • Evidence of taste, judgment, and editorial control — not just execution speed.

Note: This is not a finished product. It's a living artifact that evolves as the practice matures. New case studies will be added. The design will refine. The code will stay clean.

Reflection

What I learned from designing with AI:

AI excels at scaffolding, variation, and synthesis. It accelerates the middle of the process — not the beginning (strategy) or the end (judgment). You still have to know what you're trying to do. You still have to decide if the output is good.

The most valuable skill in AI-assisted design isn't prompt engineering. It's knowing what to ask for and when to override the answer. Prompt skill compounds with taste and judgment. Prompt skill alone is noise.

Designing with AI requires more editorial judgment, not less. The volume of output goes up; the quality bar stays the same. You're doing more curation, more filtering, more saying "no." That's not a weakness — it's where the real work is.

This portfolio is proof that you can use AI to move fast without sacrificing clarity, authenticity, or judgment. Speed is only valuable if you're moving in the right direction. That direction has to come from somewhere else.
