Case Study 05 of 05 — What I learned about senior design hiring by analyzing 6 companies

Building This Portfolio

Senior Product Designer
Personal Practice
2026
Web (Static HTML/CSS/JS)

Challenge

Six target companies. Six different evaluation rubrics. Ramp prioritizes technical collaboration and quantified impact. Figma leads with craft mastery. Anthropic splits focus between technical collaboration and impact storytelling. Meta weights scope navigation highest. A portfolio optimized for one audience actively underperforms for another.

The real problem wasn't building a portfolio — it was building a measurement system first. I needed to understand what each company actually evaluates before I could design something that holds up across all of them. Without that, every design decision is guesswork dressed as strategy.

Solution

I built the evaluation framework before I built the portfolio. Claude synthesized 50+ requirements from job descriptions at Ramp, Anthropic, Meta, Figma, OpenAI, and Cursor — extracting the specific capabilities, signals, and evidence each role required. That research became the recruiter panel: a structured evaluation system that scores portfolio work against company-specific criteria, weighted to each company's actual priorities.

The portfolio was then shaped by research, not instinct. Every case study structure, every content decision, every visual choice traced back to what the evaluation data said mattered — not gut feel.

01 — Design Decisions

Why static HTML

Code fluency is a hiring signal. Anthropic and Cursor explicitly evaluate prototyping ability. Ramp weights technical collaboration highest of any dimension. I built this site in static HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — no React, no frameworks, no templates — because the medium is part of the proof. 1,176 lines of CSS, 10 HTML pages, fully responsive, WCAG AA accessible, deployed on Vercel. No build step required.

This is not a portfolio built with a website builder. It's a portfolio built by someone who codes — and who chose raw HTML deliberately to demonstrate that range.

Visual restraint as signal

The design is intentionally spare. Dark palette, Inter typeface, teal accent (#2dd4bf), generous whitespace. Restraint signals confidence — the work speaks through case studies, not visual noise. I studied portfolios from 21 designers at target companies before landing on this direction. The pattern was clear: senior portfolios that performed well favored clarity over decoration.

Two-speed reading

Every case study is designed for two audiences at once. Bold key phrases let a recruiter extract the story in a 15-second scan. Full prose rewards a hiring manager who reads closely. This isn't formatting preference — it's a direct response to how design portfolios actually get evaluated. The first pass is always fast.

02 — AI Collaboration Model

Where AI shaped the work

Research synthesis — extracting 50+ job requirements across 6 companies would have taken days manually. Claude synthesized the themes in hours. Content architecture — I explored 4–5 different case study structures and narrative arcs per page, iterating on the core insight worth telling without writing fatigue. Code generation — the HTML/CSS backbone came from prompts I specified, reviewed, and refined. The site went from concept to deployed in weeks.

Where human judgment was the gate

Strategic direction — which stories to tell, how to position Reddit's work given confidentiality constraints, what to omit entirely. Voice and authenticity — I rewrote large passages to recover tone that got lost in iteration. Recruiting isn't marketing; the portfolio needed to sound like me. Quality judgment — knowing when "good enough" is actually good enough. AI doesn't have taste.

The governance loop

Prompt → Generate → Review → Iterate. I defined each task. Claude generated options. I reviewed for accuracy, tone, and strategic fit — then approved, redirected, or iterated. Human approval was required before any content went live. A build log tracked every session: what was generated, what was kept, what was reworked, and why.

03 — What the Panel Revealed

Scores across companies

I ran the recruiter panel — the same evaluation framework I'd built from the research — against this case study. Average score: 7.6/10 across 6 companies. Ramp scored highest at 8.2, valuing the technical collaboration evidence and quantified measurement approach. Anthropic followed at 8.1. Figma at 7.4 — they wanted more visible craft progression. Meta at 7.0 — they weighted scope navigation, which a solo project naturally lacks.

What the scores exposed

The panel confirmed what I suspected: solo projects score lower on scope navigation because there's no team to navigate, no competing priorities to broker. That's a structural ceiling, not a content problem. It also revealed that companies weighting craft mastery — Figma especially — wanted to see iteration artifacts: the rough versions, the abandoned directions, the visual progression from early to final.

The biggest liability across every evaluation was placeholder images. Case studies with image placeholders instead of real screenshots consistently scored 0.5–1.0 points lower than their content merited. The panel quantified exactly what needed fixing first.

Reflection

AI accelerates the middle of the process — not the beginning and not the end. Strategy still requires knowing what you're trying to do. Quality judgment still requires knowing whether the output is good. The most valuable skill in AI-assisted design isn't prompt engineering — it's knowing what to ask for and when to override the answer.

Building the measurement system first changed everything. Instead of designing a portfolio and hoping it worked, I designed a portfolio I could test — and the test results shaped the design. The panel isn't decorative. It's the engine.
