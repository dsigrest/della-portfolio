# Building This Portfolio

*Case Study 05 of 05 — Designing this portfolio collaboratively with AI*

## The brief

My resume and LinkedIn had been through multiple versions, but the portfolio was missing. The hiring narrative claimed AI-forward design practice, yet had no artifact-backed proof. The challenge was dual: build a portfolio demonstrating the skills senior design roles require, while using the project itself as proof point for AI-integrated workflow. The portfolio needed to close specific hiring gaps for target companies (Ramp, Anthropic, Meta, Figma, OpenAI, Cursor). Goal: Create a portfolio that IS the evidence—designed with AI, end-to-end, with transparent governance and human review gates at every decision.

## How it works

The workflow was deliberate collaboration, not black-box autopilot:

**Research synthesis:** Claude evaluated job descriptions from 6 target companies and extracted the specific capabilities and signals each role required. That framework shaped what stories to tell and how to position them.

**Content architecture:** Working through narrative structure, information hierarchy, and case study arc to tell a story about how I think—not just what I've built.

**Code generation:** HTML, CSS, and JavaScript built from scratch. No frameworks. Every line was reviewed before deploy.

**Human review gates:** At every decision point—narrative direction, tone, visual design choices, what to include and exclude—I approved or redirected. **The human decided. The AI proposed.**

This process combined rapid iteration (exploring 4–5 different case study structures, copy variants, layout options) with rigorous editorial control. Build logs tracked each session: what was generated, what was kept, what was reworked, and why. This served both accountability and continuity across work sessions.

## What AI does well / What requires human judgment

| **AI excelled at** | **Judgment remained human** |
|---|---|
| **Synthesis:** Extracting 50+ job requirements across 6 companies in hours instead of days | **Strategic direction:** Which stories to tell. How to position work given confidentiality constraints. |
| **Variation:** Rapid generation of tone variants, headline options, messaging approaches without writing fatigue | **Voice and authenticity:** Rewrote large passages to recover directness and precision. Recruiting isn't marketing. |
| **Scaffolding:** HTML/CSS/JS backbone from prompts; I specified structure, reviewed output, caught bugs, refined | **Taste:** Color palette, typography, layout. Intentionally restrained design signals confidence—a judgment call. |
| **Velocity:** High volume of options meant higher confidence in final choices by seeing alternatives | **Confidentiality:** What could be shown about past work. What needed redaction or ethical consideration. |
| — | **Quality gate:** Knowing when "good enough" is actually good enough. **AI doesn't have taste—I do.** |

**Key insight:** AI excels at scaffolding, variation, and synthesis. It accelerates the middle of the process—not the beginning (strategy) or the end (judgment). **The most valuable skill in AI-assisted design isn't prompt engineering. It's knowing what to ask for and when to override the answer.** Prompt skill compounds with taste and judgment. Prompt skill alone is noise.

**Equally critical:** Designing with AI requires more editorial judgment, not less. The volume of output goes up; the quality bar stays the same. You're doing more curation, more filtering, more saying "no." That's where the real work is.

## Built in code

**~560 lines of CSS, 6 HTML pages, responsive, accessible (WCAG AA), deployed on Vercel.** Static HTML/CSS/JS—no frameworks, no build step, no templates. This is proof that I prototype in both Figma and code depending on fidelity needs. When interaction, responsive behavior, or performance matter, I move to code.

For senior product roles that value prototyping velocity and technical literacy, this matters. Code-based prototyping is a natural extension of design practice, not a separate skill someone else owns.

- Evidence of taste, judgment, and editorial control—not just execution speed

## What I learned

**AI excels at scaffolding, variation, and synthesis. It accelerates the middle of the process—not the beginning (strategy) or the end (judgment).**
