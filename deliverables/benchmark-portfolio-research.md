## CROSS-PORTFOLIO PATTERN ANALYSIS

### Case Study Length & Structure

**Observed Ranges:**
- **Short-form:** 300-500 words + visuals (e.g., Gavin Nelson's interaction design pieces)
- **Medium-form:** 800-1200 words (most common; Ben Shih, Patrick Chew)
- **Long-form:** 1500-2500+ words (Takahiro Kawaguchi, Abdus Salam, Daniel Destefanis when applicable)

**Winning Structure Pattern:**
1. **Hook/Context** (1-2 paragraphs): What was the problem? Why was it important?
2. **Research** (visual + 1-2 paragraphs): User research, data, insights
3. **Ideation** (2-4 visuals): Sketches, concepts, iterations
4. **Solution** (3-5 visuals + narrative): Final design, key features, rationale
5. **Results/Impact** (metrics + reflection): What happened? What did you learn?

**Text-to-Visual Ratio:**
- **Optimal range:** 40% text / 60% visuals (best-performing portfolios)
- **Poor range:** 70% text / 30% visuals (feels like blog posts, not design portfolios)
- **Too visual-heavy:** 20% text / 80% visuals (hard to understand thinking/strategy)

---

### Visual Design Trends in Top Portfolios

1. **Minimalism/Restraint** (Gavin Nelson, Brian Lovin, Charlie Aufmann)
   - Whitespace, limited color, focus on typography
   - Creates perception of craft/intentionality

2. **Dark Mode as Default** (Ben Shih, Daniel Destefanis, several others)
   - Industry standard now; shows design sophistication

3. **Interaction/Animation** (Gavin Nelson, Blair Metcalf)
   - Subtle, purposeful; not gratuitous
   - Demonstrates interaction design capability
   - Shows understanding of motion design

4. **Grid-Based Layouts**
   - Majority use clean grid systems
   - Consistent spacing throughout
   - Conveys control + intentionality

5. **Typography as Hero**
   - Most use 1-2 typefaces max
   - Clear hierarchy
   - Readable (no unreadable design-for-design's-sake)

### Common Confidentiality Approaches

**Pattern:** Designers working at companies like TikTok, Google, Meta often can't show full case studies.

**Solutions observed:**
- Show "process" without revealing product details (research, sketches, systems thinking)
- Discuss approach/methodology without specific features
- Showcase design systems or internal tools (less proprietary than products)
- Use early-stage work or redesigns of public products
- Write about learning/reflections instead of project details
- Feature side projects, open-source work, or past-employer work instead

**Best example:** Daniel Destefanis compensates for limited Figma case studies by showcasing Design Lint tool (30K+ downloads, open-source proof of impact)

### What Makes Portfolios Fail

**Red Flags Absent from Top Portfolios:**
1. **Vague case studies** ("I designed a thing")
2. **No metrics/impact** (outcomes unknown)
3. **Outdated work** (oldest work is 2+ years without recent context)
4. **Orphaned confidentiality statements** ("Can't show this work" without explanation)
5. **Excessive case studies** (8+ case studies = dilution; 3-5 is sweet spot)
6. **Tool-focused instead of problem-focused** ("I used Figma and Framer" vs "I solved X")
7. **No narrative/personality** (feels corporate/generic)
8. **Inconsistent quality** (mixing student work with professional work without context)

### Portfolio Craft Recommendations

**Case Study Structure:**
- **Target:** 4-5 case studies (sweet spot; 3-6 acceptable)
- **Depth:** 800-1200 words per case study
- **Structure:** Use the Hook → Research → Ideation → Solution → Results formula
- **Specificity:** Name the problem in the first paragraph; be concrete

**Visual Presentation:**
- **Ratio:** Aim for 40% text / 60% visuals
- **Whitespace:** Generous margins; restraint in design
- **Interactions:** Include motion/prototype demos if applicable (especially for Cursor, Figma roles)
- **Typography:** 1-2 typefaces; clear hierarchy; readable
