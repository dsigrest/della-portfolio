# Writing/Thinking Page Concept

## 1. Page Name Recommendation

**Recommendation: "Writing"**

Rationale:
- "Writing" is simpler, more direct, and less pretentious than "Thinking" or "Notes"
- It's search-friendly (designers Google "designer writing" for thought leadership)
- It signals "published work," not "rough drafts" (which "Notes" implies)
- It matches the tone of the site (direct, no hedging)
- Parallel to how Brian Lovin, Julie Zhou, and other senior designers title their sections
- One word is stronger than two

Alternative considered: "Essays" (too formal for this brand). "Blog" (too informal).

## 5. Tone & Aesthetic

### Voice Rules (Matching the Portfolio)
- First person, reflective (not defensive)
- Systems vocabulary (governance, infrastructure, frameworks)
- Proof through story (concrete examples, not assertions)
- Specific over impressive (1,200 words of depth beats 4,000 words of scale)
- No hedging ("I believe this" not "some might argue")

### Visual Design
- Minimal, matches the portfolio
- Each essay is a title + subtitle + metadata (published date, read time, platform)
- No images (text only, like Brian Lovin's writing section)
- List grows over time—the page gets longer, not wider

## What This Solves

This page addresses three gaps:

1. **Thought leadership visibility** — Meta, OpenAI, Figma all flagged "no public writing" as a concern. This page signals Della documents and shares her thinking publicly.

2. **Signals depth** — Case studies show what she shipped. Essays show how she thinks. Together, they paint a complete picture.

3. **SEO & discovery** — Essays on "AI design," "design systems," "first designer strategy" will appear in searches. Portfolio case studies won't.

The Writing page is a launch pad, not a blog platform. It's curated, intentional, and quality-over-quantity. It says "I have something to contribute to the design conversation." That distinction matters.
