How do you establish trust in content you can't fully control?

Reddit wanted to consolidate fragmented SEO traffic using LLMs. Straightforward product idea. The design problem: the organization had no precedent for AI-generated content, no guidelines, no framework for handling non-deterministic output.

Trust in AI-generated content is a systems problem. Design for the range of possible outputs, not the happy path.

KLP product UI, prototype variations, edge case states

3-Layer Governance System

I designed a 3-layer governance system:

  1. Visual identification — Clear signals that content was AI-generated, woven into structure. Not a badge. A commitment to transparency.
  2. Tone and voice — Guidelines ensuring summaries were informative without falsely claiming authority. The voice reflected synthesis, not journalism.
  3. Prompt design workflow — A cross-functional review process: content design, AI engineering, product design evaluating prompts before production.

Prompt Design Workflow

Before this, prompt design was engineering-only. Product and design reviewed outputs but had no role in shaping inputs.

I established a process where product and design partnered with ML engineers to analyze prompts for accuracy, tone alignment, edge case handling, and trust signals. Each prompt was stress-tested: what happens with contentious topics? Contradicting sources? Inadvertent bias?

The review interface used progressive disclosure—reviewers drilled into failing prompts without context-switching—reducing cognitive load for high-volume review. The pattern became a template for design review systems on subsequent AI teams.

The system functioned as developer tooling: standardized how product teams interfaced with LLM capabilities. It defined input schemas, evaluation criteria, review gates. Later AI products reused this architecture.

Prompt design workflow, governance model, review criteria

Trust Decisions as Policy

AI authorship prominence — Too subtle: users didn't know they were reading AI. Too prominent: feature collapsed. The signal had to be discoverable, not obtrusive.

Attribution models — Source links vs. inline citations vs. no direct attribution. Each carried different trust implications for communities with strong attribution norms.

Contradiction handling — When AI summary contradicted sources, what should we show? Degrade it? Flag it? Surface disagreement?

Failure states — Below-threshold quality triggered what? Degraded summary? Human curation fallback? Feature removal?

The Result

Reddit's first LLM-powered product shipped. Traffic consolidated. Users engaged with unified AI-summarized pages instead of bouncing across duplicates.

The governance model and prompt design workflow became the template for subsequent AI teams, including the Answers product. The trust framework—visual identification, tone guidelines, evaluation criteria—became Reddit's starting point for all AI-generated content.

A senior engineer: "Before this, we shipped AI features and hoped users didn't notice unpredictability. After this, we designed the experience of AI."
