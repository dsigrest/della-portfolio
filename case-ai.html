Reddit Answers MVP

Senior Product Designer
SEO & Discovery
Jun 2023 – Dec 2023
Web search surfaces

Reddit had the answers — but not the answer experience

Reddit content was scattered across posts, comments, and communities. Users arriving from Google had to piece answers together themselves — and often bounced before discovering Reddit as the destination. An LLM-powered synthesis surface consolidated relevant discussions into one source-backed answer, with the original posts threaded below for verification and deeper exploration.

Competing user needs

The page had two audiences: humans and Googlebot. Humans needed readable, trustworthy answers. Googlebot needed structure, keywords, and links. Optimizing for one at the expense of the other would break the experience.

The breakthrough was framing Googlebot as a user with its own needs — not as an abstract SEO constraint. That made the tradeoffs legible to designers, engineers, and content partners, and helped the team balance discoverability with trust.

I pushed back on repeating the query throughout the page. Instead, we moved keyword signals into structured elements around the generated answer — including a pre-populated search bar that made it feel as if the user had already searched Reddit. SEO got the signal it needed, while the synthesis stayed natural and the design reinforced Reddit as the place to continue searching.

Proof before infrastructure

Dynamic generation required infrastructure investment, but the team needed evidence before committing those resources. The path forward was a lightweight proof of concept: build enough of the product to test whether the experience worked.

We started with static keyword landing pages refreshed periodically, targeting verticals with strong source coverage and high search demand. This let us test the experience, learn what quality required, and maximize impact with the resources we had.

Dual-layer page architecture

The dual-layer layout was the core design decision: synthesis on top for an immediate answer, source posts below for verification and deeper exploration. Links across both layers created clear on-ramps into Reddit while preserving the structure Googlebot needed.

The longer-term bet was to shift users from appending reddit to a Google search toward searching Reddit directly.

Principles for AI-generated search results

The page had to set expectations for a new kind of Reddit result: an answer users could scan quickly, verify through sources, and use as a starting point for deeper exploration.

Identification

We needed to define one of Reddit’s first scalable patterns for AI-generated content: what it was called, how it identified itself, and how it attributed its sources. I partnered with Brand, Legal, and Design Systems to make that pattern work across surfaces.

UI to distinguish AI-generated content

The question was bigger than visual design: what signal could make AI-generated content recognizable without making it feel gimmicky, intrusive, or opaque? User research confirmed people wanted transparency, but skepticism around AI-generated content was high. The pattern had to build trust without overexplaining itself.

We explored icons, tags, containers, disclaimers, a talking Snoo, color, and text treatments. Tags felt too heavy, Snoo felt too playful, and text-only treatments were too subtle. This crossed beyond component design into product identity: users needed to understand not just how the element looked, but what kind of Reddit content it represented.

We landed on a system, not a single solution. The final pattern combined icon, color, container, attribution, and feedback — flexible enough to adapt by surface, but consistent enough to make generated content recognizable across Reddit.

Designing the source layer

Attribution answered part of the trust problem, but users still needed to inspect the evidence behind the synthesis. User research kept surfacing the same question: where is this coming from? The source layer below the synthesis answered that directly, letting users explore the original posts and comments behind the generated answer.

I worked with data science on comment truncation and display rules. Most useful comments were short, with diminishing returns after the first few lines, so we landed on two comments per post — enough to show conversation context without overwhelming the unit.

The text truncation system reused a pattern I had already defined and validated for another surface, which helped the team move faster.

Content

Once users could recognize and verify generated content, the next question was what that content should actually say — and who it should sound like. Defining the LLM’s output was as much a design problem as defining how it looked. I led decisions around tone, clarity, and the model’s relationship to Reddit’s voice.

Voice

The team debated the role of the generated answer: was it a character, a utility, an anonymous system, or part of Reddit itself? Giving it too much personality made it feel gimmicky. Making it invisible made it hard to trust.

We landed on an omnipresent narrator without a character. The synthesis would feel like part of Reddit’s product experience — not a bot, mascot, or separate assistant.

Designing for trust

Trust depended on closeness to the source material. Broad generalizations — a consistent failure mode in early outputs — broke trust immediately. The harder challenge was synthesizing across communities without losing Reddit’s specificity or voice.

Ensuring quality

Turning quality into a repeatable process

The team had no shared criteria for what made a synthesis good: no evaluation rubric, no feedback loop, and no common language for quality. Prompt iteration was mostly vibes-based, which made it hard to tell whether outputs were actually improving.

I partnered with engineering and content design to turn subjective reactions into a structured evaluation matrix. We defined the criteria together, scored outputs against the rubric, and used the results to guide prompt and UI iteration. Involvement created buy-in.

The rubric evaluated sentence structure, source accuracy, novel synthesis across posts, length adherence, tone, content design guidelines, and SEO signals. It made quality specific enough for non-designers to evaluate consistently.

I partnered with content design to define the evaluation dimensions, then facilitated scoring sessions where the cross-functional team rated outputs against the rubric. That gave engineers, content design, and product a shared way to discuss quality and decide what to improve next.

Feedback loops

Once the rubric gave us a shared definition of quality, flagged outputs became the engine for iteration. They showed us where the model was weakest and helped the team focus each prompt, content, or UI change. The rubric evolved as we learned what users actually valued, not just what we assumed mattered.

The feedback UI became a cross-org effort: keyword landing pages, Search, and i18n were each exploring related feedback patterns. I worked across pillars to define one recognizable component users could understand across contexts. Another pattern built for this project, later adopted more broadly.

Designing graceful degradation

The same quality signals that guided iteration also determined when not to show synthesis at all. When summaries fell below the threshold, source posts became the fallback experience — giving users a useful page without showing a low-confidence answer.

The principle: degradation is not failure. Source content was the reliable baseline; synthesis was the enhancement when quality cleared the bar.

Results

The MVP proved that AI-generated answers could work on Reddit when they were source-backed, clearly identified, and held to a consistent quality bar. The system helped keep users on pages they used to bounce from, turned vibes-based prompting into a repeatable quality process, and gave Reddit a scalable precedent for AI-generated content. But the biggest impact was strategic: it clarified that synthesis belonged inside search, not on a separate search-like surface. That insight helped point the next phase toward Reddit Answers.

I handed off the foundation for the next phase: the identification library, synthesis patterns, feedback UI, evaluation rubric, and governance model. Those systems became part of the product foundation for the team that later shipped Reddit Answers.
