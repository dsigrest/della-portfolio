About

Senior Product Designer — Engagement Systems, Growth, AI

I spent 4+ years at Reddit designing engagement systems — inbox, notifications, push, email, chat — and I was usually the first designer to touch them. Five consecutive team rotations as the first (or first-in-years) designer: building vision from scratch, aligning cross-functional leadership, and shipping scalable patterns that outlasted my tenure. I joined in 2021 as an L2 designer and promoted to Senior (L4) by 2023, working directly with the Head of Design and Chief Product Officer on product strategy and vision. Along the way, I mentored junior designers on systems thinking and research-to-framework synthesis. They shipped foundational work on the platforms I helped establish and continued driving the vision forward.

My work spanned growth experimentation (push notification lifts, sharing flywheels, community activation), platform foundations (design system adoption, unified messaging surfaces), and AI-forward product design (Reddit's first LLM-powered product, prompt design workflows, content governance). I led cross-functional alignment across engineering, data science, machine learning, content design, and design systems. My pattern: join a team that either didn't exist or hadn't had design investment in years, build the strategic foundation — user problem framing, vision, scalable components, cross-functional processes — then ship and move to somewhere harder.

How I approach design

I start with the problem, not the solution. Most designers rush to pixels; I'd rather spend extra time in problem framing because that's where the high-leverage decisions live. Systems matter more than surfaces. The most impactful work I've done wasn't polished interfaces — it was invisible infrastructure: design patterns that let teams ship faster, decision frameworks that prevented misalignment, processes that made complex product questions answerable.

First designers own more than design. When you're the first on a team, you're not just designing pixels. You're building strategy, solving for alignment, establishing process, and shaping culture. You have to be comfortable being uncomfortable with ambiguity. Technology should amplify judgment, not replace it. That applies to design systems, and it applies just as much to AI. I prototype in both Figma and code (HTML/CSS/JS) depending on what fidelity the question requires — sometimes the answer isn't a mockup, it's a working prototype that tests interaction patterns or technical constraints.

I'm documenting the workflows I piloted at Reddit — prompt design review processes, design principles for non-deterministic systems, practical before/after comparisons of AI-assisted design work. This portfolio site was designed and built collaboratively with AI, and I'm writing it up as a case study in AI-native design process.

Designing for non-deterministic systems: When AI is generating content or predictions, design becomes less about controlling outcomes and more about designing trust frameworks, governance models, and evaluation criteria. How do you help users understand what they can rely on? What signals indicate the system is working? How do you handle failure modes gracefully?

The intersection of design and developer tools: Prompt design as internal tooling. Design systems as platform infrastructure. How should designers think about building tools for other builders? I'm interested in the philosophy and craft here, not just the visual design.
